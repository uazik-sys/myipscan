How to Check if Your IP Is Blacklisted

Updated: October 15, 2025

Sometimes websites, email providers, or firewalls block access because your IP address is blacklisted — listed in spam or abuse databases due to suspicious activity. Even if you did nothing wrong, your IP may share a reputation with others (for example, dynamic IP pools or shared VPN servers). This guide explains how IP blacklists work, how to check if your IP is on one, and how to get delisted safely.

What does “blacklisted IP” mean?

An IP blacklist (or DNSBL/RBL) is a database used by ISPs, mail servers, and security systems to flag potentially malicious or spammy IPs. These systems help block spam, botnets, DDoS, or compromised hosts. Blacklisting doesn’t always mean your device is hacked — sometimes entire ISP ranges get flagged for past abuse.

Common reasons for IP blacklisting

  • Sending large volumes of email (mass mailers or spam complaints)
  • Malware-infected or open proxy servers detected on your network
  • VPN or shared IPs abused by previous users
  • Misconfigured email servers (no SPF/DKIM/DMARC)
  • Port scanning or suspicious automated requests
  • Dynamic IP ranges flagged by third-party systems

How to check if your IP is blacklisted (free tools)

  1. Visit What is My IP to see your current public IP address.
  2. Copy the IP and use one or more blacklist-checking sites:
    • MXToolbox Blacklist Check
    • DNSBL.info
    • MultiRBL Check
    • Project Honeypot
  3. Review which blacklists (if any) your IP appears on. Each service provides links to removal instructions.
  4. Repeat the check occasionally, especially if you use VPNs or dynamic IPs.

How to get delisted

  1. Identify the reason: read the blacklist details (spam, malware, open proxy, etc.).
  2. Clean your system: run malware scans and remove suspicious software.
  3. Check your router for open ports or DNS hijacking.
  4. If you’re using a VPN, switch servers or contact the provider.
  5. If it’s your email server, configure SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records.
  6. Submit a delisting request (each blacklist has its own form, usually quick).

Prevention tips

  • Use a reliable VPN or ISP that rotates or manages clean IP ranges.
  • Keep systems updated to prevent compromise or open ports.
  • Avoid sending bulk or unsolicited emails.
  • Use proper email authentication (SPF/DKIM/DMARC).
  • Monitor your IP reputation periodically using the tools above.
