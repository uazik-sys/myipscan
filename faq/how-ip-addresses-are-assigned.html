How IP Addresses Are Assigned (ISP, DHCP, and IANA)

Updated: October 15, 2025

Ever wonder where your IP address comes from? Allocation flows top‑down: IANA delegates large blocks to Regional Internet Registries (RIRs), RIRs assign space to ISPs and organizations, and your device finally receives an address via DHCP/DHCPv6 or SLAAC. In this guide we unpack the layers, clarify CGNAT, and show how to tell whether your IP is static or dynamic — plus how to verify everything with MyIPScan tools.

IANA → RIRs → ISPs: the allocation chain

IANA (Internet Assigned Numbers Authority) manages the global IP pool. It allocates large IPv4/IPv6 blocks to five RIRs (AFRINIC, APNIC, ARIN, LACNIC, RIPE NCC). RIRs then assign/allocate subnets to ISPs, cloud providers and enterprises, who finally route them to end users.

How your device gets an IP: DHCP & DHCPv6/SLAAC

  • DHCP (IPv4): your router or ISP’s CPE assigns a temporary address (lease) from a local pool.
  • DHCPv6: similar for IPv6; the server hands out addresses and options (like DNS).
  • SLAAC (Stateless Address Autoconfiguration): devices construct their own IPv6 addresses using router advertisements (RAs) and network prefixes.

Many networks combine DHCPv6 for options with SLAAC for addressing.

Where NAT fits: home NAT vs CGNAT

  • Home NAT: your router translates many private IPs to one public IP for outbound traffic.
  • CGNAT (Carrier‑Grade NAT): the ISP puts many customers behind shared public IPs. This can break inbound connections and some apps; port forwarding is usually unavailable.

If you’re behind CGNAT, ask your ISP about a public/static IP or use a VPN with port forwarding when needed.

IPv6 specifics: prefixes, privacy, and multiple addresses

  • Delegated prefix: ISPs typically provide a /56–/60–/64 prefix to your router.
  • Multiple IPv6 addresses: devices may hold stable and temporary (privacy) addresses simultaneously.
  • No NAT needed: IPv6 is end‑to‑end routable; use firewalls instead of NAT for protection.

How to check what you have

  1. Open What is My IP to see your public IPv4/IPv6 and geolocation signal.
  2. Power‑cycle the router and check again. If the address changes, you likely have a dynamic IP.
  3. If inbound connections fail and your router’s WAN shows a private range (e.g., 100.64.0.0/10), you’re likely behind CGNAT.
  4. For IPv6, look for a delegated prefix on the router (e.g., /56 or /64) and verify using DNS Lookup + WebRTC Leak Test.

Privacy & security considerations

  • Use a VPN on untrusted networks to mask your public IP.
  • Keep router firmware updated; disable UPnP and unnecessary port forwards.
  • Prefer strong Wi‑Fi authentication and unique admin passwords.
  • With IPv6, rely on stateful firewalls; avoid exposing services unintentionally.
